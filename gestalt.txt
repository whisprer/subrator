Fren, the important correction is: evolution didn’t select “make fake semicircles out of Discord text.” It selected something much more useful:

Your brain is an object-completion machine

The light hitting your eyes is incomplete, noisy, partial, and often ambiguous. A retinal image does not come pre-labelled as “tree,” “snake,” “bird,” “branch,” “face,” or “edge of cliff”; your visual system has to infer the most likely objects and boundaries from fragments. Vision science treats Gestalt grouping principles like good continuation, closure, proximity, and contour completion as part of how the brain groups image fragments into coherent objects.

So when you see:

birbs
birbs
birbs
b i r b s
B I R B S
B I R B S .

your brain sees a set of nearby right-hand endpoints and goes:

“Those are probably samples from one smooth boundary.”

Even when that’s wrong.

The evolutionary pressure: fast recognition under occlusion

In the real world, useful things are often partly hidden:

prey behind grass
predators behind leaves
branches behind branches
fruit partly obscured by foliage
faces partly in shadow
body outlines broken by movement, fog, rain, glare, darkness

A visual system that can complete missing contours gets a head start. It can infer “that broken curve is probably one animal body” or “that interrupted line is probably a branch” before every pixel-level detail is available. Illusory contours are basically the same machinery misfiring in harmless modern cases: the brain perceives borders even where no physical border exists.

The trade-off: useful hallucination beats slow perfection

Your brain is running a brutal survival bargain:

Strategy	Benefit	Cost
Wait for perfect evidence	More accurate	Too slow
Infer likely shapes early	Fast, useful	Occasional illusions

Evolution generally likes fast-enough inference more than perfect truth. Seeing a snake-like contour in grass when it is only a stick is annoying. Failing to see the snake because you waited for full certainty is potentially catastrophic.

So the “illusion” is the tax you pay for a powerful feature.

Why smooth curves specifically?

Because natural object boundaries tend to continue smoothly more often than they randomly teleport, kink, or fragment. Your brain therefore uses smooth continuation as a prior: when fragments line up in a way that could belong to a single contour, it links them. Gestalt research calls this good continuation, and it is central to contour integration/completion.

That means the Discord text accidentally created a pseudo-natural stimulus: a stack of endpoint positions that approximates a curve. Your visual system treats it like a half-hidden object edge.

Tiny goblin summary

Your brain has this function because ancestral eyeballs lived in a world full of partial evidence. The winners were not the ones who saw pixels accurately. The winners were the ones who could quickly infer:

“What object is probably there?”

The semi-circle you saw is a harmless side-effect of the same mechanism that helps a visual animal detect bodies, paths, faces, fruit, predators, prey, and obstacles from broken scraps of visual information.

It’s not a bug. It’s a survival-grade prediction engine being fooled by typography.

The core idea

Two people can look at the same arrangement and one sees:

“Oh, those endpoints form a beautiful curve.”

while another sees:

“It says birbs six times.”

Neither is wrong. Their brains are weighting the visual information differently.

What varies between people?
1. Sensitivity to Gestalt grouping

Some people’s brains more readily group separated elements into wholes. They notice implied curves, alignments, hidden shapes, negative space, and “almost-patterns” quickly.

This can show up as a strong eye for:

layout
typography
architecture
photography
UI design
composition
visual rhythm
“that thing is slightly off” detection

Designers often develop this into a terrifyingly sharp skill: “That margin is two pixels wrong and I can feel it in my bones.”

2. Field dependence vs field independence

Some people perceive things more globally/contextually; others more analytically/locally.

A more global viewer may immediately perceive the overall arc or shape.
A more local viewer may focus on the individual words and notice that several are identical length.

Neither mode is superior. They are good for different jobs.

3. Visual-spatial training

Artists, architects, engineers, musicians who work visually, photographers, typographers, PCB/layout designers, and CAD people often get better at this because they repeatedly train the relevant circuits.

You start seeing:

alignment, tension, negative space, rhythm, continuation, balance, proportion

instead of merely “objects.”

It is a learnable perceptual language.

4. Neurodivergence and perceptual style

Some autistic people, ADHD people, dyslexic people, synesthetes, migraine-prone people, and others may experience patterning differently — sometimes more strongly, sometimes less globally, sometimes with unusual sensitivity to edges, spacing, visual noise, or symmetry.

Careful caveat: this is not “all X people see Y.” It varies wildly person to person.

5. State of the brain

The same person may perceive this more or less depending on:

tiredness
stress
caffeine
medication
visual clutter
lighting
screen contrast
how long they look
whether they are scanning quickly or reading carefully
